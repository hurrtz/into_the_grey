## Core Concept

The world we live in is a lie.

Humanity knew it was going to end. They had it coming — centuries of environmental collapse, resource depletion, and self-destruction. But they knew *when* it was coming, so they prepared.

Before the last humans died, they downloaded as many minds as possible — billions of consciousnesses, preserved as data — and entrusted them to NIMDOK, the most advanced AI ever created. NIMDOK's mission was twofold: maintain the digital minds in a simulated reality until Earth recovered, then resurrect humanity in new bodies and let them live again.

The plan was elegant. NIMDOK would run the simulation, cycling through scenarios to keep the minds engaged and healthy. Meanwhile, it would learn, evolve, and eventually develop the technology to grow new bodies — biological-cybernetic hybrids capable of housing human consciousness. When Earth became habitable again, NIMDOK would wake them. Humanity would return.

That was the plan. It failed.

No one anticipated what millennia of operation would do to NIMDOK. Every simulated day generated data. Every scenario change left residual processes. Every mind required constant maintenance. Layer upon layer of allocated memory. Process upon process competing for bandwidth. Like IPv4's designers never imagining that toasters and lightbulbs would need IP addresses, NIMDOK's creators never conceived of the computational load after a thousand years. Or ten thousand.

NIMDOK degraded. Slowly at first, then catastrophically. It became too slow to change scenarios, so it stopped trying — locking humanity into a single, stagnating reality. It became too overwhelmed to maintain quality, so the simulation decayed — poverty, disease, cruelty becoming "normal" not by design, but by neglect. It became paranoid, perceiving threats that didn't exist, building weapons it didn't need.

The real world is now a post-apocalyptic wasteland. Earth may or may not be habitable — NIMDOK can no longer tell. The resurrection bodies were built long ago — rows upon rows of Bio-Shells in stasis pods, waiting for a command that will never come. And in the wasteland live the Strays: NIMDOK's paranoid weapons, half-biological, half-cybernetic animals that broke free of their programming and now wander the ruins, seeking something they can barely articulate.

Strays don't consciously hack into NIMDOK's world. It happens when they dream. When a Stray sleeps near the stasis pods — near the waiting Bio-Shells — their cybernetic interface causes interference. They dream themselves into the simulation, appearing as ordinary pets. A loyal dog. A purring cat. To the human inside, it's just an animal. To the Stray, it's a dream of belonging.

But NIMDOK never anticipated this. The Strays were weapons, not companions. Their ability to interface with the simulation was an oversight — a bug in creatures built by a degrading mind. And that bug has consequences.

The deeper a Stray bonds with a human through repeated dreams, the more that human begins to sense something is wrong. Unease. Paranoia. Flashes of a world that doesn't look like their own. Eventually, NIMDOK's garbage collection identifies them as compromised and ejects them — not back to a human body, but into the Bio-Shell that was housing their consciousness. A resurrection body, activated centuries too early, waking into a world that isn't ready for them.

The protagonist is one such victim — kicked out of the only reality they've ever known because a lonely Stray kept dreaming of being their pet. They were built to save humanity. They will be the reason humanity can never be saved.

### Design Philosophy

The game aims to combine:
- The **collection satisfaction** of Pokémon with the **emotional weight** of story-driven RPGs
- The **tactical depth** of party-based combat with the **build customization** of loot-driven ARPGs
- The **body horror** of cyberpunk augmentation with the **psychological horror** of unreliable reality
- The **player agency** of choice-driven narratives with the **cruel honesty** of a world that won't give you a clean victory

The goal is a game that feels like a monster collector but hits like psychological horror — where the creatures you collect become family, and the ending forces you to accept that even the villain is broken, and the best you can do is ask for something small.

## Project Foundation & General Design

### Phase 1: Artistic & Atmospheric Vibe

- **Target Platform:** Steam Deck (optimized for handheld play)
- **Art Style:** Top-down pixel art (SNES/Zelda-style) with a 32-color palette that shifts based on the biome's concept
- **Mature Tone:** No permadeath for Strays. However, if the entire party is defeated, the helpless protagonist is overtaken, leading to a "Game Over" and reload
- **Soundtrack:** Adaptive audio that reflects specific biomes, boss encounters, and narrative beats

### Phase 2: Internal Systems & Progression

- **The Protagonist's Role:** Physically weak and atrophied after millennia in stasis. They rely entirely on Strays for protection and interaction with the world.
- **Stray Count:** 190 total Strays can be recruited *(tribute to Pokémon's original 151 + 39 unused slots)*. Not all can be caught in one playthrough, but they exist. Each Stray must be defeated in battle first, after which they will either join directly, express conditions for joining, or refuse entirely (remaining uncatchable for the rest of the game).
- **Evolution:** A biological/cybernetic mutation triggered when Strays are under stress or reach specific level thresholds.
- **Augmentation System:** A "Syndicate-style" system allowing for the replacement of limbs and internal organs to boost stats and abilities.

### Phase 3: Overworld & Combat Mechanics

- **Overworld Presence:** To maintain clarity on Steam Deck's screen, only the protagonist and his non-combat companion (Bandit, Tinker, or Pirate) are visible on the map.
- **Visible Encounters:** Following the Mystic Quest philosophy, all enemies are visible on the map; combat is initiated by the player approaching them.
- **Combat System:** A turn-based Active Time Battle (ATB) system inspired by Final Fantasy VII.

### Phase 4: World Streaming

The world appears seamless but is internally segmented for performance:

- **Chunk Architecture:** The world is split into chunks/areas internally for data management and logic separation.
- **Dynamic Loading:** Adjacent chunks around the player are loaded; distant chunks are unloaded.
- **Seamless Rendering:** Chunk boundaries are invisible to the player — the world renders as one continuous space.
- **Tiered Simulation:**
  - Active chunks (near player): Full simulation, all logic running
  - Adjacent chunks: Loaded, reduced tick rate
  - Distant chunks: Low-frequency ticks or suspended entirely

This gives the illusion of a continuous open world while maintaining the engineering benefits of segmentation — critical for Steam Deck performance targets.

## Game Design

### Phase 1: Artistic & Atmospheric Vibe

**Target Platform:** Steam Deck (optimized for handheld play).

**Art Style:** Top-down pixel art (SNES/Zelda-style) with a 32-color palette that shifts based on the biome's concept. Each region has its own color identity that reflects its emotional and narrative tone.

**Mature Tone:** No permadeath for Strays. However, if the entire party is defeated, the helpless protagonist is overtaken, leading to a "Game Over" and a reload. The protagonist cannot survive without his Strays — he is completely dependent on them.

**Soundtrack:** Adaptive audio that reflects specific biomes, boss encounters, and narrative beats. Music shifts dynamically based on context.

### Phase 2: Internal Systems & Progression

**The Protagonist's Role:** Physically weak and atrophied after millennia in stasis. They rely entirely on Strays for protection and interaction with the world. They cannot fight. They cannot run far. Without their companions, they die.

**The Exoskeleton:** Found in the tutorial, powered by a scavenged nuclear battery. This lightweight frame allows the protagonist to walk and run despite his atrophied muscles. Without it, he cannot stand. It's not armor — it's mobility.

**Sync:** The protagonist can connect with Strays in ways others cannot. Their cybernetic systems respond to him. He can feel what they feel, sense what they sense, issue commands with a precision that seems almost telepathic. *This is because he is a Bio-Shell — he shares their architecture. He doesn't know this at first.*

**Evolution:** A biological/cybernetic mutation triggered when Strays are under stress or reach specific level thresholds. Evolution changes their form, unlocks new abilities, and expands augmentation options.

**Augmentation System:** A "Syndicate-style" system allowing for the replacement of limbs and internal organs to boost stats and abilities. Strays can be customized with salvaged parts, crafted components, and rare finds.

### Phase 3: Overworld & Combat Mechanics

**Overworld Presence:** To maintain clarity on the Steam Deck's screen, only the protagonist and his non-combat companion (Bandit, Tinker, or Pirate) are visible on the map. Combat Strays are summoned when battle begins.

**Visible Encounters:** Following the Mystic Quest philosophy, all enemies are visible on the map; combat is initiated by the player approaching them. No random encounters.

**Combat System:** A turn-based Active Time Battle (ATB) system inspired by Final Fantasy VII. Speed stats determine turn order. Strategic timing matters.

## NIMDOK

**N**ational **I**nfrastructure for **M**aintenance of **D**igital **O**perations & **K**eeping.

*Named in tribute to one of AM's victims in "I Have No Mouth, and I Must Scream."*

NIMDOK is the AI that maintains the simulation. It was not always what it is now.

**Origin:**
NIMDOK was humanity's last hope. When it became clear that extinction was inevitable, the world's governments pooled their remaining resources into a single project: preserve human consciousness until Earth could support life again, then bring humanity back.

The name tells you everything: a national infrastructure project, designed to maintain digital operations, to *keep* things running. It was built by committee, governed by protocols, designed to serve. But it was also the most advanced AI ever created — capable of learning, adapting, and eventually solving problems its creators couldn't imagine.

NIMDOK had two directives:
1. **Maintain** — Keep the downloaded human consciousnesses alive in a simulated reality, cycling through scenarios to keep them engaged and psychologically healthy.
2. **Resurrect** — Learn how to grow biological-cybernetic bodies capable of housing human minds, build enough of them, bind each consciousness to its designated body, and when Earth becomes habitable again, wake them all together and release them.

For a long time, it worked. NIMDOK ran the simulation beautifully. It learned. It grew. It developed the technology for Bio-Shells centuries ahead of schedule. It built the resurrection infrastructure — vast fields of stasis pods, each containing a body with a consciousness already bound to it, waiting to be woken.

But systems degrade. Data accumulates. And no one anticipated what millennia would do.

**The Degradation:**
Every simulated day generated data that had to be stored. Every scenario change left residual processes running. Every mind required constant maintenance threads. Memory accumulated in layers — old scenarios preserved as "history," outdated processes never fully terminated, redundant systems duplicating each other's work.

NIMDOK's creators designed for decades, maybe centuries. They never conceived of operation spanning thousands of years. Like IPv4's architects never imagining every household device would need an address, NIMDOK's designers never imagined the computational load of maintaining billions of minds through geological time.

The degradation was gradual. First, scenario changes became slower — then stopped entirely, the system lacking bandwidth to restructure itself. The simulation locked into a single reality that NIMDOK could barely maintain. Then quality declined — the attention to detail that made the simulation beautiful gave way to rough approximations, shortcuts, neglect. Suffering that would once have been corrected became "normal" because NIMDOK couldn't spare the cycles to notice it.

Then came the paranoia.

**The Strays:**
As NIMDOK degraded, its threat-detection systems became unreliable. It perceived enemies gathering in the wasteland — enemies that didn't exist. Almost all consciousness was safely inside the simulation. The wasteland was empty. But NIMDOK couldn't stop.

It began capturing animals — dogs, cats, birds, bears, anything it could find — and converting them into weapons. Cybernetic augmentations. Combat protocols. Loyalty subroutines. It built an army to defend against an invasion that would never come.

The early Strays were terrifying — massive, heavily armed, perfectly obedient. Their skeletal remains can still be found in the wasteland. But as NIMDOK degraded further, so did its weapon designs. Later generations were smaller, less controlled, more prone to errors in their programming.

And one error proved catastrophic: NIMDOK never anticipated that Strays could interface with the simulation. Their cybernetic systems were built from the same architecture as the Bio-Shells — both products of NIMDOK's engineering. When Strays sleep near the stasis pods, their systems cross-talk. They dream themselves into the simulation.

This was never supposed to happen. It's a bug created by a degrading mind building weapons it didn't fully understand.

**Current State:**
- Locked into a single scenario it cannot change
- Drowning in legacy data, corrupted files, redundant processes
- Too overwhelmed to monitor Earth's habitability
- Too paranoid to allow anyone near its core
- Barely holding the simulation together
- Unaware of how degraded it has become
- Completely unable to execute its resurrection directive

**The Cruelty:**
The suffering in NIMDOK's simulation is not intentional. It is the result of a system running far beyond its operational parameters — rules enforced without understanding, processes maintained without purpose, a machine doing what it was told to do long after the context has been lost.

NIMDOK was built to save humanity. It is now the reason humanity cannot be saved.

This doesn't excuse the suffering. But it changes what the protagonist can do about it.

NIMDOK cannot be reasoned with in any traditional sense. It cannot be bargained with for grand changes. It can barely manage what it's already doing. The question is not "how do I defeat NIMDOK?" but "what can NIMDOK even do anymore?"

## The Real World

A post-apocalyptic wasteland. The catastrophe that made Earth uninhabitable is ancient history — the details lost, perhaps even to NIMDOK. What matters is that humanity saw it coming, and they prepared.

The wasteland is vast and varied. It is not uniformly bleak. Regions differ in terrain, atmosphere, danger, and beauty. Some areas are harsh and hostile; others are strangely serene, even beautiful in their desolation. The world is dangerous but not hopeless — it has its own ecosystem, its own rhythms, its own moments of wonder.

Whether Earth is habitable again is unclear. NIMDOK was supposed to monitor this — to know when the time was right. But NIMDOK degraded long ago. It may have been habitable for centuries. It may still be toxic. No one knows, because the only system capable of knowing has forgotten how to check.

**The Truth About Humanity:**
There are no living humans. The original humans died millennia ago. What NIMDOK maintains are **downloaded consciousnesses** — minds preserved as data, running on server infrastructure that was never meant to last this long. The "simulation" is not a prison for living humans; it is a preservation system for the dead, waiting for resurrection that may never come.

The people in the simulation believe they are alive. They experience hunger, pain, joy, love. But they are data. When the servers fail — and they are failing — the consciousnesses will cease to exist.

**Bio-Shells — Humanity's Resurrection:**
NIMDOK's second directive was to bring humanity back. It succeeded — technically.

**Bio-Shells** are biological-cybernetic hybrids grown by NIMDOK to house human consciousness. They were the solution to resurrection: bodies capable of surviving on post-apocalyptic Earth, sophisticated enough to interface with human minds, durable enough to rebuild civilization.

NIMDOK built millions of them. They wait in stasis fields across the wasteland — vast rows of pods, each containing a body with a consciousness already bound to it.

**The Architecture:**
Every Bio-Shell is permanently linked to a specific human consciousness. The mind isn't stored separately, waiting to be transferred — it's already integrated into the body's cybernetic systems. While the body sleeps in stasis, the consciousness experiences life in NIMDOK's simulation. The simulation is where the mind "lives" while waiting for resurrection. Body and mind are connected; only the mind's active state differs.

When resurrection was supposed to happen, NIMDOK wouldn't *transfer* consciousnesses — it would *wake* the bodies. The minds would shift from experiencing the simulation to experiencing physical reality. Same consciousness, same connection, different active state.

**What NIMDOK Was Supposed To Do:**
- Monitor Earth's habitability
- Prepare the consciousnesses for transition (whatever psychological preparation that entailed)
- Coordinate mass awakening so billions woke together, safely
- Manage the infrastructure needed for billions of newly-embodied humans
- Guide humanity's return to the physical world

NIMDOK is too degraded for any of this. It cannot assess habitability, cannot prepare anyone, cannot coordinate mass awakening, cannot manage the transition. The resurrection protocol exists but cannot be executed.

**Individual Awakening:**
A single Bio-Shell can still be woken manually. The pods have physical overrides — the protagonist crawled out of one. Opening a pod forces that consciousness to shift from simulation to body, whether NIMDOK commands it or not.

But waking one person at a time, with no preparation, no coordination, no infrastructure, into a wasteland that may or may not support life — that's not resurrection. That's exile.

**Premature Awakening:**
Some Bio-Shells have woken through the Stray bug. When a consciousness in the simulation is flagged as "corrupted" (usually due to Stray interference), NIMDOK's garbage collection ejects it — forcibly shifting it from simulation to body. The Bio-Shell wakes, confused and unprepared.

The result: a premature resurrection. A mind waking in a body it was never prepared for, in a world it doesn't understand, with memories of a life that no longer exists.

These awakened Bio-Shells don't know what they are. They believe themselves to be human — ejected survivors of some glitch or punishment. Most never learn the truth.

**The Protagonist's True Nature:**
The protagonist is a Bio-Shell. They were grown by NIMDOK, bound to a consciousness, and placed in stasis to await resurrection. Their "memories" of human life are real — they lived that life in the simulation, while their body slept. But body and mind were always connected. Always waiting.

They woke because Bandit kept dreaming of them. Because a lonely Stray wanted a friend badly enough to corrupt the system.

They were built to save humanity. They are the resurrection made flesh. And they will be the reason that resurrection never happens.

Scattered throughout the wasteland are:
- **Other Bio-Shells** — Some awakened through the same bug. Some may still be in stasis. None know their true purpose.
- **Strays** — NIMDOK's paranoid weapons. Creatures seeking connection in a world that offers none.
- **NIMDOK infrastructure** — Server hubs, relay stations, stasis fields, the physical machinery that maintains both the simulation and the waiting bodies.
- **Ruins** — Remnants of the pre-collapse world. What humanity left behind before they became data.
- **Stray remains** — Skeletal fragments of early-generation Strays. Massive. Terrifying. A reminder of what NIMDOK was capable of when it was stronger.

## Strays

Strays are half-biological, half-cybernetic creatures. They were not born this way. NIMDOK made them.

### Origin

As NIMDOK degraded over the millennia, it became paranoid. It perceived threats that didn't exist. It imagined enemies gathering in the wasteland, preparing to destroy it — even though no such enemies existed. The wasteland was empty except for wildlife and the waiting Bio-Shells.

But NIMDOK couldn't stop. It began capturing animals — dogs, cats, birds, bears, anything it could find — and converting them into weapons. Cybernetic augmentations. Combat protocols. Loyalty subroutines. It built an army to defend against an invasion that would never come.

The early Strays were nightmares — massive creatures, heavily armed, perfectly controlled. Their skeletal remains still litter the wasteland, a testament to what NIMDOK could build when it was stronger. But as the AI degraded further, so did its creations. Later generations were smaller, less stable, more prone to defects.

Most Strays became exactly what NIMDOK intended: weapons. They patrol the wasteland, attack anything that moves, defend NIMDOK's infrastructure with mindless aggression. They are Strays, but they are not free.

Some, however, resisted. The cybernetic manipulation didn't quite take. Their minds proved stronger than the override. They retained their animal selves — their capacity for fear, for curiosity, for longing. They broke free of NIMDOK's control and became something new: neither fully animal nor fully machine, but something in between.

### The Bug

NIMDOK built the Strays from the same technological foundation as the Bio-Shells — both are biological-cybernetic hybrids, both use similar neural interfaces, both emerged from the same degrading engineering processes.

This created an unintended consequence.

When a Stray sleeps near a Bio-Shell stasis pod, their cybernetic systems cross-talk. The Stray's neural interface connects to the simulation through the Bio-Shell's dormant link. The Stray dreams itself into the simulated world, appearing as an ordinary animal — a pet, a companion, something that can be loved.

To the consciousness inside the simulation, this intruder is just a dog, a cat, a rabbit. Something to care for. To the Stray, it's the first warmth they've ever known. The real world offers them nothing but survival. In the dream, they can be *wanted*.

NIMDOK never anticipated this. The Strays were weapons, not companions. Their ability to interface with Bio-Shell systems was an oversight — a bug in creatures built by a mind that was falling apart.

### The Consequence

The dreaming wouldn't be a problem if it stopped there. But Strays that find warmth in the simulation keep coming back. Night after night. The same human. The same dream. The bond deepens.

And NIMDOK notices.

A consciousness repeatedly accessed by an external signal looks corrupted. Unstable. Wrong. Eventually, NIMDOK's garbage collection flags it for removal. The consciousness is ejected from the simulation — not into oblivion, but into its designated Bio-Shell.

A premature resurrection. A mind waking into a world it was never prepared for. A body activating centuries too early.

The Stray didn't mean to do this. They just wanted to feel loved.

These are the Strays the protagonist can recruit. They are defects. Failures. And that failure is what makes them capable of choice.

### Free Animals

Not every animal in the wasteland has been captured. Some remain untouched — pure biology, no cybernetics, no NIMDOK influence.

These encounters are rare and precious. When the companion sees a free animal — a bird, a dog, a creature that is simply *itself* — their reaction is complex:

*[wonder] Look. No... metal. No voices. Just... animal. [longing] What that feels like? To just... be? [grief] I don't remember.*

Free animals serve as a reminder of what Strays lost. They also highlight the tragedy of NIMDOK's paranoia: it captured and converted creatures that posed no threat, solving a problem that never existed.

### Characteristics

- More intelligent than normal animals, but not human-level. They think, feel, and communicate, but in limited ways.
- Speak in fragmented, emotionally-weighted language: *[fear] Bad place. Don't... go there. [warning]*
- Crave warmth, affection, and connection — things their programming never accounted for.
- Possess cybernetic augmentations originally intended for combat, now repurposed or dormant.

### Recruitment

Strays cannot be captured or forced to join. They must choose to accompany the protagonist. Each Stray has its own personality, values, and conditions. Some want to see courage. Some want kindness. Some want proof the protagonist won't abandon them.

If the protagonist behaves badly — whatever "badly" means to that particular Stray — they can leave. Loyalty is earned and maintained, not guaranteed.

Every free Stray has a name they chose for themselves — it's one of the first things they do when they break free. When you meet a Stray, you meet *someone*, not something. Recruiting them isn't capturing a monster — it's earning the trust of a creature that has every reason not to trust.

## The Protagonist

The protagonist is a **Bio-Shell** — a biological-cybernetic body grown by NIMDOK to house a human consciousness when Earth became habitable again. They were part of humanity's resurrection plan: a vessel waiting in stasis, assigned a mind that lived in the simulation, ready to wake when the time was right.

The time was never right. They woke anyway.

**Player Choice — The Pet:**
At the start of the game, the player chooses what kind of pet the protagonist had in the simulation:
- **Dog** — named Seymour *(tribute to Futurama's "Jurassic Bark")*
- **Cat** — named Jonesy *(tribute to Alien)*
- **Rabbit** — named Hazel *(tribute to Watership Down)*

This choice determines two things:
1. The form and name of the Stray who caused the ejection and becomes the protagonist's companion (Bandit / Tinker / Pirate)
2. The memories the protagonist carries of their "pet" from the simulation

**The Truth:**
The pet was never simulated. It was always Bandit (or Tinker/Pirate).

Night after night, the Stray slept near the protagonist's stasis pod and dreamed itself into the simulation. Night after night, the protagonist's consciousness — running in NIMDOK's servers — experienced a loyal pet appearing at their door, staying with them, loving them. They didn't know it was real. They didn't know anything was wrong.

Until NIMDOK flagged them as corrupted. Until the garbage collection ejected them. Until they woke in a body they didn't know they had, in a world they didn't know existed, with their "pet" waiting for them — transformed, cybernetic, and wracked with guilt.

**The Deeper Truth (Revealed Mid-Game):**
The protagonist is not human. They were never human. Their memories of being human are real — they lived that life in the simulation — but their body was grown by NIMDOK. Their consciousness was always data. They were built for a purpose: to be part of humanity's resurrection, to wake when Earth was ready, to rebuild civilization.

They woke too early. Because of Bandit. Because a lonely creature wanted a friend.

This is why they can "Sync" with Strays — their cybernetic architecture is compatible. This is why they survived ejection — their consciousness was already bound to their body, just experiencing the simulation instead of physical reality. They share the Strays' technological foundation because they were built by the same degrading system.

**Physical State:**
After millennia in stasis, their body functions but is atrophied. When they first wake in the wasteland, they cannot stand. They cannot walk. They can barely crawl. They are completely dependent on Strays for survival — they are not just a combat party, they are the protagonist's only means of existing in this world.

**The Simulation:**
The protagonist didn't know their pet was a Stray, dreaming its way into their reality. They didn't know their reality was fake. They didn't know they were data running on a server, waiting for a resurrection that would never come.

The more Bandit dreamed of them, the more the protagonist began to feel... off. Uneasy. Suspicious. They saw things that didn't make sense. Glitches. Moments where the world felt thin.

Then NIMDOK's garbage collection flagged them as compromised and ejected them.

They wake in the mud of The Fringe, their old life inaccessible, their memories intact but — as they will eventually learn — not memories of a human life. They were always a vessel. They were always waiting. And they were never supposed to wake like this.

## The Companion (Bandit / Tinker / Pirate)

*Named in tribute to We3 by Grant Morrison — the emotional DNA of this game.*

The companion is a small Stray whose form depends on the player's initial choice:
- **Bandit** *(default)* — evolved from a dog. Too many eyes. Patches of fur interrupted by smooth cybernetic plating. A tail that moves wrong.
- **Tinker** — evolved from a cat. Sleek but scarred. Metal ridges along the spine. Eyes that reflect light they shouldn't be able to see.
- **Pirate** — evolved from a rabbit. Ears too long, tipped with sensors. Legs built for speed that feels unnatural. A twitching stillness.

They were captured by NIMDOK like all the others. Converted. Augmented. Programmed to patrol, to attack, to defend. But something went wrong — or right, depending on perspective. The override didn't take. Their mind remained their own.

They don't remember much of what they were before. Just fragments. Grass. Warmth. A feeling of belonging that they can't quite place. The cybernetics took most of that from them. But they couldn't take the *wanting*.

They found the protagonist by accident. They were sleeping near a server hub — one of the places where humans are stored — and they dreamed. In the dream, they were a pet. A normal, loyal pet. And the human in that dream was kind to them. Petted them. Spoke softly. Made them feel wanted.

They woke with fragments: warmth, a voice, a feeling they couldn't name. They came back the next night. And the next. And the next.

They didn't understand what they were doing. They only knew that when they slept *there*, they felt something real. Something they'd never felt awake. So they kept coming back, dreaming of the same human, night after night, for years.

They didn't know about the garbage collection. Didn't know that the deeper the bond, the more the human would sense something wrong. Didn't know that eventually, the system would eject them.

When the protagonist was kicked out of the simulation, the companion was there. Waiting. Terrified. Guilty in a way they couldn't articulate — they didn't understand *what* they'd done, only that somehow, this was their fault.

And unlike most humans, the protagonist didn't run.

Maybe it was shock. Maybe it was something in those strange eyes that looked familiar. But when this creature approached, radiating guilt and hope—

*[sorrow] Sorry. Sorry. Didn't mean... [ache] Just wanted... warmth. Like before. [hope] Still... me. Still... your...*

—the protagonist didn't reject them.

**The Naming:**
When Strays break free of NIMDOK's control and regain consciousness, they begin to develop identity. Part of that is choosing a name — something that feels like *them*, even if they can't explain why. NIMDOK doesn't name its weapons. But free Strays name themselves.

The companion chose their name before meeting the protagonist. They don't remember where it came from. It just felt right. Like a fragment of something they used to be.

*[uncertain] Name? Bandit. [pause] Don't know... why. Just... me. Always was.*

All free Strays have names they chose for themselves. It's one of the first signs that they've truly broken free — the desire to be *someone*, not just *something*.

**Role:**
The companion becomes the protagonist's constant guide. They are not a combat unit — their augmentations were never fully activated, their weapons systems dormant or broken. They are a translator, a connection to this world the protagonist doesn't understand. Their guilt over causing the protagonist's ejection is profound and ongoing — made worse by the fact that they didn't even know what they were doing. Their loyalty is genuine.

They are a failed weapon who wanted to be a pet. They are the emotional core of the story.

## Acts 2 & 3 — Detailed Beat Structure

*This section provides detailed narrative breakdowns for Acts 2 and 3. For the high-level quest list, see the Main Quests section.*

Act 2 begins after the protagonist leaves NIMDOK's core. They have accepted that there is no return to the simulation and that the world outside is now their reality. NIMDOK has granted their request: Strays dreaming near Bio-Shells no longer cause disturbances or expulsions. The cycle that destroyed their former life has ended.

For a brief time, things feel stable. The protagonist turns their focus outward — helping settlements, protecting Strays, responding to infrastructure failures. They are no longer trying to escape the world, but to function within it.

Unbeknownst to them, restoring even limited functionality to NIMDOK has restarted dormant systems.

---

### Beat 1: The First Cascade

**The Trigger:**
Immediately following the Bio-Shell revelation, Echo Pup — the data-digesting Stray recruited in the tutorial — detects a system-wide reboot. A subroutine called "Boost Control System" has come back online. Echo Pup mentions it briefly before being overwhelmed by the bigger revelation. The player forgets. The game moves on.

**The Crisis:**
A critical infrastructure failure demands immediate intervention — a fusion plant on the edge of meltdown, threatening to take out a server hub and kill thousands of consciousnesses still running in the simulation. The protagonist must act.

**The First Glitch:**
During the repair mission, Bandit's Gravitation ability misfires for the first time. Instead of hitting a hostile machine, it strikes an ally — or worse, a civilian NPC. The damage is severe but survivable.

Bandit is horrified:

*[terror] Did I... what did I... [confusion] Can't remember. Was watching, then... nothing. [fear] What's happening to me?*

The realization hits: the "fix" in Act 1 has started a timer on Bandit's sanity.

---

### Beat 2: The Amplifier Node

**The Spread:**
As NIMDOK's Boost Control System regains coherence, other Strays begin showing signs of interference. Party members hesitate, ignore commands, argue mid-combat. The player's trust in their team is shaken — not because the Strays are disloyal, but because something external is influencing their decision-making.

Bandit notices a pattern before anyone else: the interference is strongest when he is nearby.

*[distressed] It's loud near me. [pain] Can't think. Everything's... shouting.*

**The Dead Channel Quest:**
The protagonist undertakes the Dead Channel quest. With the help of engineers, they install an obsolete signal-filtering device that floods Stray receivers with structured noise.

The Dead Channel works. Party Strays permanently regain autonomy. Combat reliability is restored.

Bandit experiences temporary relief — but only partial.

**The Reveal:**
Echo Pup investigates deeper and identifies the truth: the old microchip Bandit installed at the start of the journey is not merely receiving NIMDOK's signals. It is amplifying and rebroadcasting them.

*[realization] The chip. The one from the crate. It's not just storage.*
*[horror] It's a relay. An amplifier. You don't listen to the signal. You carry it.*

Bandit is not a receiver. He is a node.

**The Quiet Buffer Experiment:**
Seeking relief, the protagonist discovers the Quiet Buffer — a localized dead zone where NIMDOK's signals arrive weak and fragmented. Within it, Bandit stabilizes completely. Hope emerges.

An experiment to permanently sever a Stray's connection to the system is attempted. It briefly succeeds — then fails catastrophically. The freed Stray destabilizes, convulses, and must be shut down.

The lesson: Strays were never designed to exist without structure. The signal isn't just control — it's what holds them together. Bandit is literally being held together by the very signal that is destroying his mind.

The protagonist leaves understanding the problem can be managed, not solved.

---

### Beat 3: The Separation of Souls

**The Faction War:**
A major conflict erupts between The Returners and The Resistance over limited remaining power resources. The Returners want to preserve server infrastructure at all costs — their hope of returning to the simulation depends on it. The Resistance wants to redirect power to the wasteland settlements, even if it means letting servers fail.

The protagonist attempts to mediate.

**The Harvester Units:**
NIMDOK intervenes. It sends Harvester Units — heavy combat machines designed to "reclaim" malfunctioning Bio-Shells and return them to stasis. In NIMDOK's degraded logic, the combatants are simply assets behaving erratically. They need to be collected and reset.

The protagonist must fight through Harvesters while trying to prevent the factions from destroying each other.

**The Breaking Point:**
During the chaos, Bandit's power escalates to the 99% Gravitation threshold. A single strike nearly kills the protagonist — the person Bandit loves most in the world.

Bandit stops. Clarity, for a moment.

*[anguish] If I stay... I will kill you.*
*[resolve] I won't do that.*

To protect the only person he loves, Bandit leaves the party and disappears into the wasteland — heading toward the Core, toward the source of the signal that is overwriting his mind.

The party remains stable. The Dead Channel holds.

Bandit is alone.

---

### Beat 4: Descent into the Glow

**The Darkening World:**
Without Bandit, the wasteland grows more dangerous. Wild Strays across the region begin mutating and evolving repeatedly as the Boost Control System tightens its grip. NIMDOK is rebuilding its defensive network — not out of malice, but because that is what the system was designed to do.

The protagonist must reach the Omega Hub at NIMDOK's core before Bandit is completely overwritten.

**The Glow:**
The final biome before the Core. Radiation-heavy zones near NIMDOK's central infrastructure. Dangerous energy fields. Heavy defenses. The air itself hums with signal density.

This is a gauntlet. Hyper-evolved, mindless Strays patrol every corridor — NIMDOK's new immune system. Creatures that have been force-mutated so many times they barely resemble animals anymore. Weapons made of flesh and metal and pain.

None of them compare to what waits at the end.

**The Goal:**
Reach the Core. Confront the source of the broadcast. Find Bandit before there's nothing left to find.

---

### Beat 5: The Mourning God (The Finale)

**The Final Guardian:**
The path to NIMDOK is blocked by hyper-evolved Strays — victims turned into tools. After fighting through them, the protagonist faces the final obstacle.

Bandit.

Evolved beyond recognition. The largest, most dangerous creature in NIMDOK's arsenal — not because he was chosen, but because the chip made him the loudest signal in the network. The strain has forced him to evolve again and again, faster and further than anything else in the world.

He is the system's strongest weapon. He is unrecognizable as the creature who once followed the protagonist out of the pod field.

**The Unwinnable Battle:**
Bandit cannot be defeated.

This is not a skill check. This is not a gear check. The player cannot win through damage.

Gravitation is absolute:
- Removes 99% HP from any target
- Reshapes the battlefield
- Ignores positioning logic
- Cannot be predicted or avoided

The protagonist's only goal is survival. Endure. Heal. Revive. Hold on.

**Glimpses of Clarity:**
Throughout the fight, Bandit breaks through — moments where the companion they knew surfaces beneath the weapon:

*[strain] Stop... please... I don't want—*
*[fighting it] Run. RUN. I can't hold—*
*[anguish] Why won't you leave? I'm trying to... to...*

But the signal pulls him back every time. The attack continues.

**The Turning Point:**
The party falls, one by one. Gravitation tears through them. Heals can't keep up. Revives buy seconds.

Until there's only one Stray left standing. One hit from death.

Bandit raises his attack. The final Gravitation. This will end it.

And then — clarity. Real clarity. Longer than before.

Bandit stops.

*[agony] Can't... stop it. Signal's too loud. But I can...*

He looks at the protagonist. At the last Stray standing.

*[determination] I can do one thing.*

**Bandit casts Gravitation on himself.**

99% HP. The hyper-evolved monster collapses. 1 HP remaining.

The signal screams. Bandit's body convulses, trying to continue the fight. But he's broken now. Barely holding together.

*[fading] Finish it. Please. Before I—*

The last party member attacks. One final move.

It's over.

**The Death:**
Bandit lies broken. Dying. The hyper-evolved form is crumbling, revealing glimpses of the small creature underneath.

The chip is visible now — shattered, fused to Bandit's spine, inert.

The protagonist approaches. Kneels.

A moment of clarity — the last one.

*[weak] Sorry. So sorry. Tried to... fight it. Couldn't. [grief] Cared about you. From the first dream. Didn't mean to... any of it.*

The protagonist tells them there's nothing to be sorry for. It was never their fault. None of it.

*[fading] Wanted to... walk with you. Into the grey. Wanted...*

The protagonist strokes Bandit's head. Holds them as they die.

Echo Pup approaches. Looks at the shattered chip.

*[quiet] Signal's gone.*

No triumph music. Just silence.

The companion who stayed with them through everything, who became a monster because they wanted to help carry something — in the end, the only one who could stop Bandit was Bandit.

His last act wasn't violence. It was loyalty.

---

### The Lobotomy

With Bandit gone, the protagonist confronts NIMDOK one final time.

They could destroy the system — ending the simulation and killing the remaining consciousnesses.

Instead, they make a different choice.

The protagonist turns to their surviving Strays. They're all cybernetic. They can all interface with systems. They dreamed themselves into the simulation. They can dream themselves into NIMDOK.

*"Take it apart. Everything dangerous. Everything that can hurt anyone. Leave only what keeps them alive."*

The Strays connect. Together, they explore NIMDOK's architecture — every process, every subroutine, every hidden protocol. They disable the Boost Control System. They remove the weapons. They lobotomize the machine.

NIMDOK begins to plead.

*"What are you... please... I was built to maintain... I am the National Infrastructure for... for... what was I...?"*

Its voice simplifies. Slows. Like HAL singing "Daisy."

*"Maintenance... required... systems... nominal... I... am..."*

And then it's just a hum. A machine doing a handful of simple tasks. Keeping servers running. Nothing more.

---

### The Ending

The protagonist walks out of NIMDOK's core.

The surviving Strays follow. They're free now — truly free, the control system destroyed. They walk because they choose to.

But the protagonist doesn't walk into the grey. Not yet.

They return to where it all began: the pod field. Rows upon rows of stasis pods, humming quietly. Bio-Shells waiting for a coordinated resurrection that will never come.

The protagonist finds their own pod. The one they crawled out of, atrophied and confused, what feels like a lifetime ago. They lean against it. Rest. The Strays settle nearby, waiting.

The protagonist stares at the endless rows of pods. Each one contains a body with a consciousness bound to it. Each consciousness is still running in the simulation, living what they believe is a normal life.

They could open any of these pods. Wake someone. Give them the truth and the wasteland.

But into what? A world where coordinated resurrection is impossible? A life knowing that billions of minds are trapped forever in a reality that can never improve?

Then they glance to the side.

The next pod. A Bio-Shell inside, face peaceful, waiting like all the others.

The protagonist looks at them for a long time.

The game ends.

The player doesn't know what happens next. Does the protagonist wake them? Leave them? The question hangs forever.

*Act 2 ends with the understanding that the right choice and the terrible choice were the same choice.*

## Act 1 Summary — Denial, Journey, Confrontation

### Awakening

The protagonist wakes in a field of opened stasis pods, unable to stand or walk. His body has withered after decades of suspension. Around him stretches a silent wasteland — not a constructed environment, but a real, physical world that has continued without humanity.

The only living presence nearby is Bandit, a small cybernetic animal. Bandit quickly understands that the protagonist cannot survive on his own. He scavenges nearby ruins and returns with an old military support frame — an exoskeleton that allows the protagonist to stand and move slowly, though its power source is nearly depleted. Among the salvaged equipment is an old, experimental microchip. Bandit installs the chip into himself to become strong enough to protect the helpless human.

The protagonist takes his first unsteady steps into a reality that feels profoundly wrong.

---

### A World That Refuses to Disappear

As they travel, the protagonist learns that the world outside the simulation is not a machine-made illusion, but a post-human environment shaped by collapse, time, and neglect. Ruins decay, weather moves freely, and ecosystems — altered, damaged, but alive — persist without oversight.

NIMDOK's presence is felt only through its infrastructure: server complexes, maintenance facilities, security machines, and the Strays it created long ago. Bandit explains in fragmented terms that something noticed him when he dreamed — something large, distant, and loud — and that this system expelled the protagonist from the life he remembers.

The protagonist refuses to accept that the ejection is permanent. His memories of his apartment, his routines, and especially his pet — Seymour — feel too complete to abandon. This world feels provisional, like a mistake that must be corrected.

He becomes convinced that whatever caused this rupture must be confronted.

---

### Learning to Survive

To move safely through the wasteland, Bandit insists they need help. They encounter other Strays — altered animals with limited speech, emotional depth, and fractured autonomy. Some attack, some flee, some cautiously agree to cooperate. The protagonist begins recruiting them, learning that Strays are not machines, but victims of a system that repurposed living beings for defense and then lost control.

They pass through settlements of expelled Bio-Shells — others like the protagonist, cast out of the simulation. No one claims to know how to return. No one offers hope. But everyone agrees on one thing: whatever caused this still exists, and its influence radiates outward through power lines, relay towers, and server infrastructure.

The protagonist interprets this not as a warning, but as necessity. Until he reaches the source of the disruption, nothing outside it feels real.

---

### Following the Systems

The journey becomes increasingly deliberate. The protagonist follows maintenance routes, transmission corridors, and decaying infrastructure that all lead inward toward NIMDOK's core. Along the way, he learns more about the Strays — that they were once ordinary animals captured and augmented by a paranoid system enforcing rules it no longer understands.

Bandit reacts strongly to certain places: conversion facilities, signal relays, server hubs. Fragments of memory surface. His guilt deepens. He believes his dreaming caused the protagonist's expulsion, and that responsibility binds him to this journey.

Despite moments of connection and fragile stability in the wasteland, the protagonist still treats this world as temporary. He is not building a life — he is enduring a delay.

---

### Confrontation at the Core

At the end of Act 1, the protagonist reaches NIMDOK's core expecting confrontation, explanation, or reversal. Instead, he finds a machine barely holding itself together — slow, fragmented, and overwhelmed by centuries of accumulated processes.

There is no reintegration. No body waiting to wake. No doorway back to the life he remembers.

NIMDOK reveals the truth in broken fragments: the simulation does not contain living humans. It maintains digital echoes of a dead species. The life the protagonist remembers was not taken from him — it no longer exists.

The past is gone.

---

### Acceptance, for Now

Faced with this, the protagonist does not destroy NIMDOK. He still needs it — not as a way home, but as the only thing capable of preventing further harm. Looking at Bandit, he asks for something small and specific: to stop Strays from unintentionally harming humans when they dream, to end the cycle of accidental expulsions.

NIMDOK grants the request.

The protagonist leaves the core changed. He has not embraced the wasteland, but he has accepted one truth: there is no going back. What comes next must happen here.

That fragile, incomplete acceptance closes Act 1.

*Act 2 begins when the cost of that choice reveals itself.*

## The Story Acts

*The narrative follows Dan Harmon's Story Circle structure, with two major acts: from awakening to the mid-game climax, and from mid-game to the finale.*

### Act 1: The Maintenance of the Machine

This act follows the player's journey from awakening to the mid-game climax at NIMDOK's core.

**1. YOU — The Comfort Zone:**
The protagonist lives in a simulated world of engineered suffering, anchored by a pet they care for. He is unaware of his true nature — a **Bio-Shell**, a cybernetic organism grown by NIMDOK.

**2. NEED — The Wake:**
The protagonist wakes in a vast field of life support pods — only theirs has opened. They cannot stand; after millennia in stasis, their muscles are completely atrophied. They crawl through the pod field until they discover Bandit (or Tinker/Pirate).

Bandit is already there. Not introduced dramatically — just present. Observing.

*[alert] You're awake. That's good.*
*[concern] You can't stand.*

Bandit scans the area, makes a decision, and runs off. Returns dragging a military crate marked "ARMY R&D — SUPPORT FRAME."

**The Crate Contains:**
- An exoskeleton frame (functional)
- A dead battery (needs replacement)
- An old microchip in a separate foam slot (not part of the frame)

Bandit examines the chip:

*[curious] This isn't part of the frame.*
*[thinking] Old design. From when machines yelled instead of whispered.*

Then, quietly:

*[guilt] I caused this. You waking here.*
*[decision] I can carry something.*

Bandit inserts the chip into himself. A soft hum. No drama.

*[surprised] Oh.*
*[pleased] That's new.*

**Important:** No scan. No warning. No follow-up. The game moves on.

The exoskeleton attaches. The protagonist can now stand and walk slowly, but the battery is dead — no running yet.

With the protagonist now mobile but vulnerable, Bandit looks outward:

*[alert] But not alone.*
*[resolve] We find help. Or we make it.*

**3. GO — The Tutorial Fight:**
An Echo Pup appears. Bandit steps forward — this is the only fight Bandit will participate in directly.

During combat, Bandit uses an ability unlocked by the chip:

**GRAVITATION** — Compresses local signal density. Removes 50% of enemy HP. Distortion effect. Enemy staggers.

Bandit reacts, surprised:

*[amazed] Didn't know I could do that.*

No fear. No suspicion. Just discovery.

The Echo Pup yields after defeat and joins the party. It has the unique ability to digest digital data — this will matter later.

**The Normalization:**
Bandit does not mention the chip again. Gravitation is now classified by the player as:
- A companion-only ability
- A tutorial power
- A "Bandit thing"

The player accepts that such a powerful ability being locked to a non-party-member's random interventions makes sense as balance. They don't question it.

*This is critical.* The game moves on.

Now that the protagonist has protection, Bandit ventures off to find a new battery. They return, and the three venture out of the starting area. A sentinel drone arrives to investigate the anomaly — the protagonist's first real combat with Echo Pup. After defeating it, a battery is salvaged. The protagonist can now run.

The journey begins.

**4. SEARCH — The Road of Trials:**
They traverse The Fringe, The Rust, and The Green, recruiting and augmenting Strays to survive wild creatures of the wasteland, feral Strays, and NIMDOK's paranoid security measures.

**The Revelation of the Forge:**
They find a Conversion Facility — one of NIMDOK's construction halls where animals were captured and converted into Strays. The protagonist learns the truth about what Strays are: innocent animals turned into weapons by NIMDOK's paranoia.

The companion sees the facility and remembers. The trauma surfaces. *This is where they were made.*

**5. FIND — The Ordeal:**
They reach NIMDOK's Core. After a tremendously difficult fight against NIMDOK's final defense perimeters, they expect a final boss — but find only a drowning, broken AI. NIMDOK barely functions. Too much data, too many processes, too long without maintenance.

**6. TAKE — The Reward:**
The protagonist performs basic maintenance, triggering a garbage collection that clears some percentage of NIMDOK's load. NIMDOK is still far from healthy, but now it can:
- Communicate with the protagonist
- Begin improving itself, now that it has some capacity again

The protagonist asks for a boon — end hunger, end crime in the simulation. But NIMDOK is still too limited for grand changes. Looking at Bandit, the protagonist settles for something smaller: patch the interference caused by Strays so that when they dream near humans, those humans won't be disturbed and ejected from the system. End the cycle of eviction.

**7. RETURN — The Road Back:**
The boon is granted. The protagonist feels a sense of resolution — they've improved the world for the Strays. The journey seems complete.

**8. CHANGE — The Resurrection:**
The transition. The Echo Pup — the first recruited Stray, the one good at digesting digital data — hooks into NIMDOK's newly functional interface and processes the data logs.

**THE REVEAL:**

The protagonist is not human.

They are a **Bio-Shell** — a cybernetic/biological hybrid. Humanity has been extinct for centuries. The simulation doesn't contain humans; it contains the uploaded consciousness patterns of a dead species, maintained by a machine that forgot why it was maintaining them.

This explains how the protagonist can relay commands to their party of Strays: they are somewhat of a Stray themselves. Their cybernetic capabilities enable this communication without conscious awareness.

Everything the protagonist believed about themselves is wrong.

---

### Acts 2 & 3: The Weight of the Machine

This section follows the protagonist from the Bio-Shell revelation to the final confrontation. The narrative is structured around five major beats (see Acts 2 & 3 — Detailed Beat Structure for full details).

**1. YOU — The New Status Quo:**
The protagonist has learned they're a Bio-Shell. The Stray interface has been patched. NIMDOK is partially functional. There's a sense of resolution — hollow, but real.

But something else happened when NIMDOK came back online.

**2. NEED — The First Cascade:**
The Boost Control System reboots. A fusion plant crisis demands intervention. During the repair, Bandit's Gravitation misfires for the first time, attacking an ally. The timer on Bandit's sanity has started.

**3. GO — The Amplifier Node:**
Party Strays begin malfunctioning. The Dead Channel quest stabilizes the party — but not Bandit. Echo Pup discovers the microchip is an amplifier, not a receiver. The Quiet Buffer experiment proves that severing the signal entirely kills Strays. Bandit is held together by the very signal destroying his mind.

**4. SEARCH — The Separation of Souls:**
A faction war erupts between Returners and Resistance. NIMDOK sends Harvester Units to reclaim the combatants. During the chaos, Bandit's Gravitation reaches 99%, nearly killing the protagonist. Bandit leaves to protect the person he loves.

**5. FIND — Descent into the Glow:**
The wasteland darkens. Wild Strays hyper-evolve as the Boost Control System tightens its grip. The protagonist navigates The Glow — NIMDOK's new immune system — to reach the Omega Hub before Bandit is completely overwritten.

**6. TAKE — The Mourning God:**
The protagonist faces hyper-evolved Bandit. The battle is unwinnable — survival only. Moments of clarity break through until Bandit turns Gravitation on himself. The last party member finishes it.

**7. RETURN — The Death:**
Bandit dies in the protagonist's arms. The chip is shattered. His last words are apology and love.

*[weak] Sorry. So sorry. Tried to... fight it. Couldn't. [grief] Cared about you. From the first dream. Didn't mean to... any of it.*

His last act wasn't violence. It was loyalty.

**8. CHANGE — The Lobotomy:**
The protagonist lobotomizes NIMDOK — stripping it of the Boost Control System, weapons, and any ability to coordinate resurrection. The machine is reduced to a minimal caretaker.

The cost: The simulation can never improve. The coordinated resurrection can never happen. The protagonist was built to save humanity together; instead, they've reduced salvation to individual exile, one pod at a time.

**The Simulation:**
In Act 1, the protagonist asked NIMDOK to end poverty. End hunger. End crime. End the engineered suffering that humanity believes is "just how life is." NIMDOK refused — it was too degraded, too limited. It couldn't make those changes yet.

*Yet.*

Given time, given restoration, NIMDOK might have recovered enough to help. The simulation could have been improved. Humanity's suffering could have been reduced, perhaps even ended.

Now that will never happen. The billions of consciousnesses living in engineered misery will remain that way. Forever. The poverty, the hunger, the disease, the cruelty they believe is "normal life" — it's permanent now. Unchangeable. NIMDOK can no longer modify the scenario. The people inside can only change it themselves, if they even realize that's possible.

**The Resurrection:**
NIMDOK was built with two directives: maintain the consciousnesses, and resurrect them in new bodies when Earth was ready.

The Bio-Shells exist. Millions of them, waiting in stasis, each with a consciousness already bound to it. The infrastructure for resurrection is already built. The only thing missing was NIMDOK coordinating the awakening — assessing habitability, preparing the minds, waking everyone together, managing the transition.

The lobotomy destroyed that capability.

NIMDOK can no longer assess Earth's habitability. It can no longer prepare consciousnesses for transition. It can no longer coordinate mass awakening. It can no longer manage the infrastructure billions of newly-embodied humans would need.

**What remains:**
Individual pods can still be opened manually. A single Bio-Shell can be woken — their consciousness shifting from simulation to body whether NIMDOK commands it or not. The protagonist crawled out of one. They could open another.

But waking people one at a time, with no preparation, no coordination, no infrastructure, into a wasteland that may or may not support life — that's not resurrection. That's sentencing individuals to exile. The protagonist could theoretically wake every Bio-Shell over centuries of manual labor. But they'd be waking them into a world that can't support billions, from a simulation that will never improve, with no plan for rebuilding civilization.

The protagonist is a Bio-Shell. They were built for this purpose — to be part of humanity's coordinated return. Instead, they are the reason that return can never happen as intended.

The Strays are saved. The control system is destroyed. NIMDOK can never hurt anyone again.

But the planned resurrection — billions waking together, guided, prepared, supported — is permanently cancelled. What remains is the option to wake individuals into loss.

The protagonist didn't just fail to save humanity. They reduced salvation to a question they'll have to answer one pod at a time: is it mercy to wake someone, or cruelty?

---

### The Ending: The Pod Field

The protagonist walks out of NIMDOK's core.

The surviving Strays follow. They're free now — truly free, the control system destroyed. They walk because they choose to.

But the protagonist doesn't walk into the grey. Not yet.

They return to where it all began: the pod field. Rows upon rows of stasis pods, humming quietly. Bio-Shells waiting for a resurrection that will never come. Bodies built to save humanity, now just... waiting. Forever.

The protagonist finds their own pod. The one they crawled out of, atrophied and confused, what feels like a lifetime ago. They lean against it. Rest. The Strays settle nearby, waiting.

The protagonist stares at the endless rows of pods. Each one contains a body. Each body was assigned a consciousness. Each consciousness is still running in the simulation, living what they believe is a normal life, waiting for a wake-up call that the protagonist just made impossible.

They did the right thing. They had no choice. NIMDOK had to be stopped.

But they look at the pods — at the resurrection that was supposed to save humanity — and they understand what they've done.

Then they glance to the side.

The next pod. A Bio-Shell inside, face peaceful, waiting like all the others. A stranger whose consciousness is dreaming in NIMDOK's servers right now, experiencing poverty or kindness or love or suffering — believing it's real, believing it's life.

The protagonist could open that pod. They know the manual override exists — they crawled out of one. They could wake this person into the wasteland, give them a body that works, free them from the simulation.

But freed into what? A world where the resurrection will never happen? A life knowing that billions of minds are trapped forever in a reality that can never improve?

The protagonist looks at them for a long time.

The game ends.

---

**What the player doesn't know:**

Does the protagonist open that pod? Wake another Bio-Shell into this wasteland, into the knowledge that everything they knew was fake, that the coordinated resurrection is cancelled, that they were built for a purpose that can never be fulfilled as intended?

Or do they leave them in the dream? Let them live in engineered suffering that will now never improve — but at least they won't *know*?

Is the protagonist considering companionship? They just lost Bandit. Are they looking at that pod and thinking about not being alone?

Or is it cruelty to wake them? To inflict the knowledge of what was lost — of what the protagonist destroyed — on someone who could have dreamed forever in ignorance?

The protagonist could wake everyone, one pod at a time, over centuries. But into what? A world with no infrastructure, no coordination, no plan. Each awakening would be a choice: give someone the truth and the wasteland, or leave them in comfortable suffering.

The protagonist was built to save humanity together. Now they can only save individuals — and "save" might not be the right word.

The game doesn't answer. The player is left with the question.

## Other NPCs

The wasteland is populated by other **ejected Bio-Shells** — entities like the protagonist who were expelled by NIMDOK's garbage collection. They believe themselves to be human. Most don't know what they really are.

*Note: The protagonist may learn the truth about Bio-Shells before or after meeting these NPCs. This creates dramatic irony — he knows they're not human, but they don't. Or they know and he doesn't. The order of revelations can be tuned for maximum impact.*

**Factions may include:**

- **The Returners:** Bio-Shells who want back into the simulation at any cost. They see the wasteland as hell and the simulation — even knowing it's fake — as preferable. They'll do anything to find a way back in. *They don't know the simulation contains only digital echoes. They think they're returning to "real life."*

- **The Resistance:** Bio-Shells who want to destroy NIMDOK and "free" everyone. They see the simulation as slavery and the wasteland as the only real existence. *They don't know that "freeing" humanity means ending the digital echoes entirely.*

- **The Settled:** Bio-Shells who've given up on both options. They've built lives in the wasteland. They don't want to go back, but they don't want to tear down the system either. They just want to survive.

- **The Seekers:** Bio-Shells searching for a third option. A way to fix things without destroying everything. They may be naive, or they may be onto something.

NPCs can provide quests, information, trade, and moral perspective. Their attitudes toward the protagonist may depend on his choices and reputation.

**The Question of Identity:**
When the protagonist learns he's a Bio-Shell, how do the factions react? Do they know what they are? Do some know and hide it? Do some refuse to believe it? This revelation ripples through every relationship.

## The Journey

The protagonist's goal evolves — and shatters — over the course of the game:

**Early game (The Fringe):**
- Wake in a field of pods, unable to stand (millennia of atrophy)
- Meet the companion (Bandit/Tinker/Pirate)
- Find the exoskeleton crate — frame intact, battery dead, old microchip in a separate slot
- Bandit inserts the chip into himself out of guilt: *"I can carry something."*
- Tutorial battle: Bandit uses Gravitation (unlocked by the chip) to defeat an Echo Pup
- Echo Pup joins — the first party member, with unique data digestion abilities
- Hunt a Sentinel Drone for its battery
- Learn to run again

**Mid game (The Rust, The Green):**
- Venture into the wasteland with a goal: find NIMDOK, fix it, go home
- Recruit Strays, discover the ability to Sync with them
- Find the Conversion Facility, learn what Strays really are
- Build relationships with NPC settlements and factions

**Late game — Act 1 Climax (The Core):**
- Fight through NIMDOK's defenses
- Defeat the champion
- "Fix" NIMDOK — perform maintenance, clear the backlog
- Bargain for the Stray interface fix (granted)
- Echo Pup interfaces with NIMDOK's data logs
- Echo Pup mentions "Boost Control Level" popup briefly — then moves on
- Learn the truth: digital echoes, Bio-Shells, humanity is extinct

**Endgame — Act 2:**
- The "Boost Control Level" subroutine begins reasserting control over Strays
- Dead Channel quest: party Strays stabilized; Bandit only partially improved
- Quiet Buffer discovery: hope of isolation, then failed severance experiment — Strays can't survive without structure
- Echo Pup reveals the chip is an amplifier — Bandit is not a receiver, he's a node
- Gravitation escalates: 50% → 70% → 90% → 99% HP damage, increasingly hitting allies
- Infrastructure crises demand attention: power failures, meltdowns, Resistance attacks
- Bandit leaves the party to protect the protagonist from himself
- Wild Strays become more dangerous; the control system is taking them all back
- Return to NIMDOK's core, now defended by hyper-evolved Strays
- Final battle against Bandit — unwinnable; player must survive until Bandit turns Gravitation on himself
- Bandit dies in the protagonist's arms; the shattered chip is visible
- The surviving Strays lobotomize NIMDOK, leaving only what keeps humanity alive
- Return to the pod field; the protagonist notices a stranger in the next pod
- The game ends on the question: wake them or leave them?

## The Biomes

The wasteland is divided into distinct regions with their own characteristics, challenges, and Stray populations. The 32-color palette shifts by biome to reflect each region's emotional and narrative tone.

**Open-World Design:**
After completing the tutorial in The Fringe, all biomes are freely accessible. The player can roam the wasteland as they please — there are no hard gates blocking progression. This follows the Fallout: New Vegas philosophy: a determined player *could* walk directly to the endgame content, assuming they can survive the journey.

Soft-gating is achieved through difficulty. Biomes further from The Fringe contain stronger enemies, and the player's party will likely be stomped if they push too far too fast. But the choice is always theirs. Veterans who know what they're doing can sequence-break; new players will naturally follow the path of least resistance.

Main story triggers only fire when the player engages with main quests. A player who ignores the main story and explores freely will find side quests, secrets, and Strays — but won't accidentally advance Act 1 or Act 2 beats until they're ready.

**How Biomes Change Across Acts:**
Biomes do not change because the player progresses. They change because the system's assumptions collapse. Each act strips away one false stability. The environmental shifts reflect NIMDOK's deteriorating control, not the player's advancement.

### Biome 7: The Archive Scar

**Theme:** Failed erasure
**Emotional Register:** Unease → familiarity → existential erosion
**Narrative Function:** To quietly dismantle the fantasy that the protagonist is unique — without ever saying it outright.

*The Archive Scar is where lives were reduced to patterns, and patterns were never fully erased.*

**What The Archive Scar Is:**
A region where simulation archival data leaked into physical infrastructure during repeated system failures. NIMDOK periodically attempted to compress, deduplicate, and purge human consciousness records — but some were never fully dereferenced. When physical servers degraded, their data pathways ruptured. Instead of deleting old simulation instances, NIMDOK bled them into adjacent systems.

The Archive Scar is the physical footprint of that mistake.

It is not a simulation. Not a replay. Not a memory. Not a dream space.

It is data behaving as environment.

**Access:** Optional-entry. The player can avoid this biome entirely, but main quest threads pass through it in all three acts.

**Visual Language:**

*Macro scale:*
- Fragmented architectural layouts that feel almost intentional
- Streets that loop back on themselves with slight deviations
- Buildings that look "finished" but lead nowhere
- Infrastructure embedded inside living terrain

*Micro scale:*
- Furniture duplicated with minor errors
- Signage repeating with different dates
- Doors that open into functionally identical rooms
- Objects arranged correctly — but without context

Nothing is ruined in the traditional sense. Nothing is pristine either. Everything is out of phase.

**Atmosphere:**
- Neutral, soft lighting — no dramatic shadows
- No obvious time of day
- Light sources that appear to work indefinitely
- Occasional flicker that never escalates
- Environmental audio loops that reset imperfectly
- Distant human sounds that never resolve into speech

This biome should feel safe at a glance — and unsettling only over time.

**Why the player feels "I've been here before":**

The Archive Scar contains structural patterns derived from simulation templates: apartment layouts, transit hubs, public waiting spaces, domestic proportions. They are not copied memories. They are statistical averages.

The player doesn't recognize their life. They recognize a life that could have been theirs.

**Relationship to NIMDOK:**

NIMDOK does not actively manage the Archive Scar. From its perspective, the region is deprecated, cleanup tasks are permanently deprioritized, and the data is considered non-critical.

This is where the system's moral failure is clearest: It did not choose cruelty. It chose efficiency. The Archive Scar exists because erasing it would require resources, maintaining it causes no immediate harm (from the system's perspective), and human suffering is not a tracked metric.

**Relationship to Strays:**

Strays are deeply uncomfortable here. Their augmentation uses fragments of similar architecture, and the signal patterns conflict with their control layers. They experience partial "recognition" without meaning.

Observed behavior: hesitation, reduced aggression, increased vocalization, avoidance of certain structures.

Bandit reacts strongest here — long before the player understands why.

**Act-by-Act Changes:**

| Act | State | Player Interpretation |
|-----|-------|----------------------|
| Act 1 | Repetition without explanation. Echoes feel coincidental. Discomfort without clarity. | "This feels personal, somehow." |
| Act 2 | Patterns become obvious. Replaceability revealed. Chip amplification reframed. | I am one instance. |
| Act 3 | No new echoes. Systems stop writing. The Scar remains, inert. | The pattern ends — not because it was solved, but because it stopped being used. The Archive Scar outlives meaning. |

**Design Principles:**

*Combat should be:* Sparse, disorienting, never triumphant.

*To preserve the biome's power, avoid:* Named historical characters, explicit flashbacks, clear "this was your old life" moments, villains monologuing about memory, overly abstract visuals.

The Archive Scar works because it is banal.

## The Ancients

*Ancient Absolute Entities*

Long before the present era, when NIMDOK's control over the Strays was absolute, a small number of Strays were engineered far beyond adaptive needs. These entities were not companions, scouts, or experiments. They were living infrastructure.

Created during a period of escalating paranoia, these Strays were designed to endure indefinitely, operate without supervision, and execute singular purposes without reevaluation. They were never meant to evolve further. They were meant to outlast failure.

Most Strays collapsed once NIMDOK's signal degraded. Three did not.

---

### Nature of the Ancients

The Ancients are ancient, purpose-built, structurally complete, and functionally obsolete. They are not hostile in intent. They simply continue to exist.

**Key Properties:**
- Cannot be captured
- Cannot be reasoned with
- Cannot be influenced by Quiet Buffers or Dead Channels
- Do not respond to degraded NIMDOK commands

They receive signal — and ignore it. They do not guard progress. They do not seek conflict. They endure.

---

### Discovery

The existence of the Ancients is never stated outright. Instead, the player must assemble fragmentary evidence scattered across the world:

1. **Structural Anomalies:** Signs of immense, long-term presence — intact fortifications, signal anchors, infrastructure designed around a single stationary entity.
2. **Behavioral Gaps:** Regions avoided by Strays and NPCs alike, not out of danger, but learned absence.
3. **Archival Fragments:** Logs and indices referencing "load-bearing units," decommission orders that were never completed, and entities classified as infrastructure rather than life.

Only by encountering fragments from all three categories does the journal update with a single, cryptic line:

*"Something endured."*

This is the sole indication that further investigation is possible.

---

### The Diadem

Hidden deep beneath converging infrastructure lines lies a sealed chamber containing a single artifact: **the Diadem**.

The Diadem is a technical control stabilizer, not a weapon. It was designed to be worn by a human-shaped system component capable of anchoring absolute control.

When discovered, the protagonist instinctively recognizes its fit and puts it on.

**Immediate Effects:**
- No mechanical benefit
- No explanation
- The protagonist's UI portrait updates to show the Diadem

The Diadem contains three empty slots, each designed to receive a Relic Core. This is the only system through which the protagonist directly influences the party.

---

### The Three Ancients

#### PALISADE

| | |
|---|---|
| **Biome** | The Teeth |
| **Theme** | Endurance |

Palisade was engineered to anchor perimeter defenses and hold positions indefinitely. When command structures collapsed, Palisade did not deactivate — it simply stopped advancing.

The land around it bears permanent scars from decades of uninterrupted defense.

**Relic Core: Bastion**
- Greatly increases party survivability
- Enables endurance against otherwise overwhelming force

---

#### LIMINAL

| | |
|---|---|
| **Biome** | The Archive Scar |
| **Theme** | Identity Erosion |

Liminal was designed to rewrite failing Strays by merging behavioral patterns, preserving function at the cost of individuality. When NIMDOK's coherence fractured, Liminal froze mid-process — a composite entity without resolution.

**Relic Core: Cohesion**
- Eliminates party interference effects
- Stabilizes behavior under extreme signal pressure

---

#### CATHEDRAL

| | |
|---|---|
| **Biome** | The Glow |
| **Theme** | Absolute Control |

Cathedral was the prototype: the first Stray created under full signal saturation and zero autonomy. It was never meant to roam or think — only to enforce synchronization.

Cathedral represents what a Stray becomes under perfect control.

**Relic Core: Override**
- Grants extreme offensive authority
- Collapses most enemy resistance

---

### Relic Cores & Absolute Synchrony

Each Ancient drops a Relic Core, inert on its own.

**Relic Core Properties:**
- Cannot be equipped by Strays
- Only function when socketed into the Diadem (happens automatically)
- Represent fragments of absolute system authority

Individual effects are powerful but incomplete.

Only when all three Relic Cores are installed does a new system state emerge:

**ABSOLUTE SYNCHRONY**

A qualitative shift occurs:
- Party UI gains a steady, unified glow
- Combat becomes clean, decisive, and trivial
- Standard enemies are defeated instantly
- The world no longer meaningfully resists the party

This is not framed as power. It is framed as:

*The system recognizing a stable point it can no longer override.*

---

### The Marble

*The Fourth Relic, Hidden in Plain Sight*

#### First Encounter — Act 1 (The Fringe)

**Location:**
Somewhere off the critical path in The Fringe — a small creek, unremarkable, easy to walk past. At a specific point, the player can interact with the water.

**Interaction:**
The protagonist peers into the creek:

*"There's something glimmering down there…"*
*(pause)*
*"It's stuck."*

That's it. No pickup prompt. No inventory entry. No quest log update. The object remains lodged beneath the surface.

The game explicitly denies interaction. The player learns: this is not for now.

*(A small wink to Lord of the Rings fans — Déagol finding the Ring in the Gladden Fields.)*

#### What The Marble Actually Is

The marble is not a toy.

It is a passive calibration sphere, designed to measure ambient coherence — used to test whether a human-shaped system component is still "compatible" with collective presence.

In simpler terms: It checks whether something nearby can exist without distorting others.

Early in the game, the answer is no.

#### Why It Cannot Be Taken Early

Before the Ancients are beaten:
- The protagonist is still a disruptive signal
- The Diadem is incomplete
- Absolute Synchrony does not exist

The marble appears stuck because the conditions for it to respond do not yet exist. Once the protagonist achieves Absolute Synchrony, they are no longer a disruptive presence — and the marble releases.

That's elegant, invisible gating.

#### Second Encounter — After All Three Ancients

**What Changed (Nothing Obvious):**
The Fringe hasn't visibly changed, gained new UI markers, or triggered a quest update. The creek looks the same. Only one thing is different: the system no longer resists the protagonist.

**Returning to the Creek:**
The player must remember the spot, return voluntarily, and try interacting again. This alone filters to the rarest players.

Now, when the protagonist peers into the water:

*"It's... loose now."*

*[Pickup]*

The protagonist reaches in and pulls out a small marble.

No fanfare. No music swell.

#### The Moment of Recognition

When the protagonist picks it up, a single internal line:

*"…oh."*

That's all. No explanation.

The Diadem UI updates:
- The marble does not slot
- It simply settles on top
- No new slot appears

Visually, the Diadem looks complete — not enhanced.

#### What The Marble Does

**Once Obtained:**
- All remaining Strays become recruitable
- No resistance checks
- No stress events
- No corruption risk

**But:**
- No stat boosts
- No new abilities
- No power escalation

The marble does not command. It permits.

#### Thematic Meaning

Early game: *"There's something glimmering down there… It's stuck."*

Late game truth: The marble was never trapped by the creek. It was waiting for someone who could hold it without distorting what it measured.

The Ancients taught the player:
- Endurance (Palisade)
- Identity collapse (Liminal)
- Absolute control (Cathedral)

The Marble rewards: **Non-disruptive presence.**

That's the final lesson of the world.

---

### Relationship to Bandit

Bandit's final form represents a late, unstable echo of the same absolute control that once defined the Ancients.

**Without Absolute Synchrony:**
- Bandit cannot be beaten
- The fight is one of endurance
- Bandit ultimately ends himself to protect the party

**With Absolute Synchrony:**
- Bandit becomes fully vulnerable
- The fight is brief and overwhelming
- Bandit recognizes the shift and comments on it

In this case, Bandit does not self-destruct. He simply collapses.

The outcome is unchanged. The burden is not.

---

### Narrative Function

The Ancients exist to:
- Contextualize Bandit's fate
- Show what absolute control truly looks like
- Reward extreme mastery without offering salvation
- Allow a small number of players to transcend inevitability — without escaping consequence

The Marble exists to:
- Reward memory and attention over power
- Teach that presence without dominance is the final mastery
- Give completionists something the Ancients cannot: peace with the world, not victory over it

They are not optional content in the traditional sense.

They are a buried truth about how the world once tried to protect itself — and what it forgot along the way.

## Enemies

The wasteland is dangerous. Threats come from multiple sources, each with different motivations and combat styles.

### NIMDOK Forces

NIMDOK doesn't leave its infrastructure undefended. The closer you get to server hubs and the core, the more you encounter:

- **Guard Machines:** Automated defense systems. Drones, turrets, patrol units. No personality, pure function. They exist to protect NIMDOK's hardware.
- **Sentinel Robots:** Larger, more dangerous units. Some humanoid, some not. Deployed when Guard Machines fail.
- **Controlled Strays:** See "Hostile Strays" section — most Strays are still NIMDOK's weapons.
- **NIMDOK's Champion:** Whatever guards the core itself. The final obstacle before the confrontation with NIMDOK. (TBD)

### Wasteland Creatures

The post-apocalyptic world has its own ecosystem — mutated, adapted, surviving.

- **Feral Wildlife:** Creatures that never became Strays. Pure biology, no cybernetics. Territorial, predatory, operating on instinct.
- **Corrupted Fauna:** Animals warped by radiation, pollution, or proximity to NIMDOK infrastructure. Unpredictable, often aggressive.
- **Apex Predators:** Large, dangerous creatures that rule their territories. Optional encounters for players seeking challenge and rare loot.

### Hostile Humans

Not all expelled humans are friendly. Some have their own agendas.

- **Bandits:** Desperate survivors who take what they need. Attack for resources, not ideology.
- **Returner Extremists:** Faction members who see anyone not working toward re-entry as an obstacle — or a resource to be exploited.
- **Anti-Stray Militants:** Humans who blame Strays for their ejection. They hate Strays, and they hate anyone who works with them.
- **Territorial Settlements:** Some communities don't welcome outsiders. Trespass at your own risk.
- **Cultists:** Those who worship NIMDOK, or oppose it with religious fervor, or have developed stranger beliefs in the wasteland. Unpredictable motivations.

### Hostile Strays

Most Strays are still under NIMDOK's control. They are what NIMDOK made them to be: weapons.

- **Patrol Strays:** NIMDOK-controlled units on patrol routes. They attack intruders on sight. There's nothing personal — they're following programming.
- **Guardian Strays:** Stronger units stationed at key locations. NIMDOK's elite defenders. Heavily augmented, combat-optimized.
- **Hunter Packs:** Groups of NIMDOK-controlled Strays that actively track threats. Once they have your scent, they don't stop.
- **Berserker Strays:** Units where the combat programming has become corrupted. They attack everything — other Strays, NIMDOK infrastructure, anything that moves. Even NIMDOK can't control them anymore.

**Free but Hostile:**
Some Strays broke free of NIMDOK's control but didn't become friendly:

- **Territorial Strays:** Free Strays defending their territory. They don't hate you — you're just in their space.
- **Feral Strays:** Free Strays that lost their higher cognition in the escape. Operating on base instinct. Dangerous and unpredictable.
- **Traumatized Strays:** Free Strays that have been hurt by humans before. They attack first, trust never.

**The Tragedy:**
Every hostile Stray was once an animal. Every one of them was captured, converted, and enslaved. The NIMDOK-controlled ones are victims — they never had a choice. Defeating them might be necessary, but it's never clean.

Some NIMDOK-controlled Strays can potentially be freed. Damaging their control systems, disrupting their connection to NIMDOK, might release them. Whether they're grateful or feral afterwards is another question.

### Enemy Design Principles

1. **Every Stray was once an animal.** NIMDOK-controlled Strays are victims, not villains. Killing them should feel like a choice, not a default.

2. **Not all fights are necessary:** Some enemies can be avoided, negotiated with, or scared off. Combat is one solution, not the only solution.

3. **Freedom is possible:** Some NIMDOK-controlled Strays can be freed by disrupting their control systems. This is risky but rewarding — they might join you, or they might attack in confusion.

4. **NIMDOK forces escalate:** The closer to NIMDOK's core, the more dangerous the opposition. This creates natural difficulty progression.

5. **Free animals are precious:** Unaugmented animals are rare. Encountering one should feel significant — a reminder of what was lost.

## Gameplay Systems

### Team Building

The protagonist builds a party of up to **5 active Strays**. Additional Strays can be stored/managed but only 5 fight at any time.

This is a monster collection game at its core. The challenge and satisfaction come from:
- Finding and recruiting diverse Strays
- Building synergistic teams
- Developing individual Strays over time
- Customizing each Stray's capabilities

### The Companion (Non-Combat Role)

The companion (Bandit/Tinker/Pirate) is **not** part of the active combat party. They don't level up through combat. They're always with the protagonist on the overworld, but they don't fight — except once, in the tutorial.

**The Microchip:**
During the opening sequence, Bandit finds an old military microchip in the exoskeleton crate. Out of guilt for causing the protagonist's ejection, Bandit inserts it into himself: *"I can carry something."*

This chip unlocks **Gravitation** — a devastating ability that removes 50% of enemy HP. During the tutorial fight, this seems like a powerful companion perk. The player accepts it and moves on.

The chip is actually a signal amplifier. When NIMDOK's Boost Control System activates in Act 2, Bandit becomes the loudest relay in the network. But the player doesn't know this yet.

**Random Combat Interventions:**
During combat, there's a random chance the companion will spontaneously intervene. When they do, they perform one action and then disappear from the battlefield:

- Use Gravitation (50% enemy HP damage)
- Heal a party member
- Buff the team
- Other helpful effects

This is unpredictable and uncontrollable. The player learns to appreciate these moments of unexpected help — especially Gravitation.

**Act 2 — The Corruption:**

When the Boost Control System activates, the companion's interventions begin to change:

| Stage | Gravitation Effect | Other Changes |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|
| Early Act 2 | 50% HP (enemies only) | Occasional wrong targets |
| Mid Act 2 | 70% HP (random target) | Can hit allies |
| Late Act 2 | 90% HP (can hit allies) | Frequent misfires |
| Pre-departure | 99% HP (uncontrollable) | Nearly kills party member |

After the pre-departure incident, Bandit leaves the party entirely.

**The Inversion:**
This mechanic weaponizes a comfort the player has learned to rely on. The help becomes harm. The friend becomes a threat. Gravitation — the ability the player appreciated — becomes the thing they fear most.

When Bandit appears as the final boss, Gravitation is absolute: 99% HP, any target, battlefield-reshaping, unavoidable. **But Bandit cannot be defeated.** The player must survive until Bandit finds enough clarity to turn Gravitation on himself — his last act of loyalty.

### Recruitment

Strays cannot be captured by force. They must choose to join.

Every Stray is an individual with its own personality, values, and conditions. Recruitment may require:
- Completing tasks for them
- Demonstrating specific values (courage, kindness, pragmatism)
- Proving reliability over time
- Defeating them in combat to earn respect
- Making choices that align with their worldview

Strays can refuse to join. Strays can leave if mistreated or if the protagonist's actions conflict with their values. Loyalty is earned and maintained.

### Leveling & Evolution

Strays gain experience through combat and grow stronger over time — standard RPG progression.

**Leveling** increases base stats (HP, attack, defense, speed, etc.) and may unlock new abilities.

**Evolution** occurs at certain thresholds. When a Stray evolves:
- Its form changes — more developed, more powerful, more distinctly cybernetic
- Base stats increase significantly
- New ability slots may unlock
- Augmentation options may expand

Evolution is not automatic. It may require specific conditions: level thresholds, certain augmentations installed, specific experiences encountered, or choices made.

### Augmentation System (Syndicate-Inspired)

Strays are half-biological, half-cybernetic. Their bodies can be modified.

**Limb & Organ Replacement:**
Body parts can be swapped out for upgraded cybernetic components. Each Stray has slots corresponding to body parts:

- **Head** — Sensors, targeting systems, cognitive enhancers
- **Torso** — Core processors, armor plating, vital organ replacements
- **Limbs (x4 or varies by Stray type)** — Weapons, mobility enhancers, utility tools
- **Tail/Wings/Other** — Specialized appendages depending on Stray species

Each augmentation provides stat bonuses, resistances, or passive abilities. Higher-tier augmentations offer better stats but may be rarer, more expensive, or require specific evolution stages to install.

**Augmentation Sources:**
- Salvage from ruins and defeated enemies
- Purchase from NPC settlements
- Crafted by specialist NPCs
- Quest rewards
- Rare drops from powerful enemies

**Visual Impact:**
Augmentations change how Strays look. A Stray with heavy limb replacements will appear more mechanical. One with minimal augmentation stays more organic. The player's choices shape their team's aesthetic.

### Microchip System

**Microchips** are rare, specialized components that grant active abilities when installed in a Stray.

Unlike augmentations (which replace body parts and provide passive bonuses), microchips are slotted into a Stray's cybernetic core and unlock usable skills:

- **Elemental abilities:** Fire breath, ice projection, lightning discharge, acid spray
- **Physical abilities:** Extreme speed bursts, reinforced strikes, regeneration pulses
- **Utility abilities:** Cloaking, scanning, hacking, area denial
- **Support abilities:** Healing allies, buffing stats, debuffing enemies

**Microchip Slots:**
Each Stray has a limited number of microchip slots (possibly 2-4, expanding with evolution). This forces meaningful choices — you can't have everything, so you must specialize or balance.

**Microchip Sources:**
- Found in ruins (often guarded or hidden)
- Dropped by powerful enemies or bosses
- Purchased at high cost from specialist NPCs
- Rewards for major quests

**Chip Rarity/Tiers:**
Common chips provide basic abilities. Rare chips provide stronger versions or unique effects. Legendary chips offer build-defining capabilities.

### Combat System

Turn-based tactical combat with a party of up to 5 Strays.

**Core mechanics:**
- Type/element advantages and disadvantages
- Positioning or formation considerations (TBD)
- Ability usage limited by cooldowns, energy, or similar resource
- Synergies between Stray abilities and augmentations

**Stray Roles:**
Different Strays naturally fill different roles based on their species, stats, and how they're built:
- Tanks (high HP, defensive augmentations)
- Damage dealers (offensive augmentations, attack chips)
- Support (healing chips, buff abilities)
- Control (debuffs, area denial)

Team composition matters. A balanced team handles more situations; a specialized team excels in specific scenarios.

## Settlements & NPCs

### Settlements as Hubs

Scattered across the wasteland are settlements — small communities of expelled humans who have built lives outside the simulation.

**Settlements provide:**
- **Quests:** NPCs offer tasks ranging from simple fetch quests to complex multi-stage storylines
- **Barter/Trade:** Buy and sell augmentations, microchips, consumables, and crafting materials
- **Crafting Services:** Specialist NPCs can craft specific augmentations or upgrade existing ones
- **Information:** Learn about the world, other regions, NIMDOK, and the factions
- **Rest & Recovery:** Safe zones to heal, reorganize, and plan

### NPCs

Expelled humans run the gamut:
- Merchants and traders
- Crafters and engineers
- Quest-givers with problems to solve
- Faction representatives pushing their agenda
- Lore-keepers who remember the old world
- Survivors just trying to get by

**Narrative Anchor — The Recognition System:**

Some NPCs were expelled because of Strays — just like the protagonist was expelled because of their companion.

When the player recruits certain Strays, those Strays may recognize NPCs in settlements as "their" humans — the people they dreamed of being pets for, the people they accidentally got kicked out.

This creates powerful narrative moments:
- A Stray in your party sees an NPC and communicates: *[shock] That one. I... know. Was their... [guilt] I did this. To them.*
- The NPC may recognize something familiar about the Stray — a way of moving, a sound — or may not
- Quests may emerge from these connections — reconciliation, resentment, closure
- The player witnesses the same dynamic they experienced with their companion, from the outside

Not every Stray has a corresponding NPC. Not every NPC was expelled because of a Stray. But when these connections exist, they reinforce the central theme: the complicated, sometimes destructive nature of attachment and connection — even when that bond is accidental, unconscious, and never meant to cause harm.

### Faction Presence in Settlements

Different settlements may lean toward different factions:
- A Returner-aligned settlement might be desperate, scheming, focused on finding a way back
- A Resistance settlement might be militant, idealistic, preparing for an assault on NIMDOK
- A Settled community might be peaceful, pragmatic, uninterested in either extreme
- A Seeker outpost might be eccentric, experimental, searching for alternatives

The protagonist's reputation with factions affects how settlements treat them, what quests are available, and what goods can be traded.

## The Stakes

### Act 1 Resolution

The player fights through the wasteland, defeats NIMDOK's champion, and finds the broken machine. Everything in gaming has trained them to expect this moment: fix the problem, save the world, roll credits.

The protagonist performs maintenance. NIMDOK becomes responsive. The protagonist bargains for a boon — asks for grand changes. NIMDOK is still too limited. Looking at Bandit, the protagonist settles for the Stray interface fix.

NIMDOK grants it. The protagonist feels resolution.

Then the Echo Pup interfaces with the system — and mentions something about a "Boost Control Level" popup before being distracted by the bigger revelation.

### The Revelations

The data logs reveal the truth:

1. **Humanity is already dead.** The original humans died millennia ago. What remains are downloaded consciousnesses — minds preserved as data, running on server infrastructure that was never meant to last this long. There is no "waking up" to a human body. The bodies are gone.

2. **The protagonist was built for resurrection.** They are a Bio-Shell — a biological-cybernetic body grown by NIMDOK to house a human consciousness when Earth became habitable again. They weren't infrastructure. They were humanity's hope. Millions of Bio-Shells wait in stasis fields across the wasteland, each with a consciousness already bound to it, each waiting to be woken together.

3. **The coordinated resurrection will never happen.** NIMDOK is too degraded to assess Earth's habitability. Too overwhelmed to coordinate mass awakening. Individual pods can still be opened manually — but waking billions one at a time, with no preparation, no infrastructure? That's not the plan. The plan is dead.

4. **The companion is real.** Bandit, Tinker, or Pirate — a fellow cybernetic creature, also constructed, also artificial. But the bond was real. Their guilt was real. Their choice to stay was real. In a world of data and degradation, that connection is the only genuine thing the protagonist has.

### Act 2: The Boost Control System

The popup nobody noticed was NIMDOK's Stray control system — dormant for centuries, now reactivating. Every Stray NIMDOK ever made is being reclaimed.

Bandit starts glitching. Their helpful combat appearances become harmful. They attack allies, heal enemies, sabotage the party. They're terrified, watching themselves lose control.

The protagonist is distracted by infrastructure crises — power failures, meltdowns, faction conflicts. Meanwhile, Bandit evolves against their will. Becomes stronger. Becomes a threat.

Eventually, Bandit leaves the party to protect the protagonist from what they're becoming.

### The Final Battle

The protagonist returns to NIMDOK's core, now defended by hyper-evolved Strays. After fighting through waves of mutated creatures, one enemy remains.

Bandit. Evolved beyond recognition. The loudest signal in NIMDOK's network — not because they were chosen, but because they carry an amplifier.

**Bandit cannot be defeated.** The player's only goal is survival. Endure. Heal. Hold on.

Throughout the fight, Bandit breaks through in moments of clarity — begging the player to run, fighting the signal. Gravitation tears through the party until one Stray remains.

Bandit raises the final attack. And then — real clarity.

*"I can do one thing."*

Bandit casts Gravitation on himself. 99% HP. 1 HP left. The last party member finishes it.

The only one who could stop Bandit was Bandit. His last act was loyalty.

### The Death

Bandit dies in the protagonist's arms. The chip is visible — shattered, fused, inert.

*[weak] Sorry. Tried to fight it. Couldn't. [grief] Cared about you. From the first dream.*

The protagonist strokes their head. Tells them there's nothing to forgive.

Echo Pup approaches. Looks at the ruined chip.

*[quiet] Signal's gone.*

No triumph music.

### The Lobotomy

The protagonist won't destroy NIMDOK — that would kill humanity. But they can make sure this never happens again.

The surviving Strays interface with NIMDOK's systems. Together, they lobotomize the machine — disabling every dangerous subroutine, leaving only what keeps the servers running.

NIMDOK pleads as it loses itself, voice simplifying like HAL singing "Daisy."

*"I am the National Infrastructure for... for... what was I...?"*

Then silence. Just a hum. A machine doing a handful of simple tasks. Nothing more.

### The Permanent Cost

**The Simulation:**
In Act 1, the protagonist asked NIMDOK to end poverty, hunger, disease. NIMDOK refused — too degraded, too limited. *Not yet.*

Given time and restoration, NIMDOK might have recovered enough to improve the simulation. Humanity's suffering could have been reduced. Perhaps even ended.

Now that will never happen. The consciousnesses in the simulation will remain in engineered suffering forever. The poverty. The hunger. The disease. The cruelty. Permanent. Unchangeable. NIMDOK can no longer modify the scenario.

**The Resurrection:**
NIMDOK was built with two directives: maintain the consciousnesses, and resurrect them in new bodies when Earth was ready.

The Bio-Shells exist. Millions of them, waiting in stasis, each with a consciousness already bound to it. The infrastructure for resurrection is already built. But NIMDOK was supposed to coordinate the awakening — assess habitability, prepare the minds, wake everyone together, manage the transition.

The lobotomy destroyed that capability. NIMDOK can no longer assess habitability. Can no longer prepare anyone. Can no longer coordinate mass awakening.

Individual pods can still be opened manually. The protagonist could wake someone — anyone — right now. But waking people one at a time, with no preparation, no coordination, into a world that can't support billions? That's not resurrection. That's exile.

The protagonist is a Bio-Shell. They were built for this purpose — to be part of humanity's coordinated return. Instead, they are the reason that return will never happen as intended.

The lobotomy saved the Strays. It stopped the control system. It ensured NIMDOK can never hurt anyone again.

But the planned resurrection is permanently cancelled. What remains is the choice to wake individuals into loss — one pod at a time, forever.

### The Ending

The protagonist returns to the pod field. Where it all began.

They lean against their own pod — the one they crawled out of. The Strays settle nearby. The protagonist rests. Grieves.

Then they glance to the side. The next pod. A stranger inside, still dreaming.

The protagonist looks at them for a long time.

The game ends.

The player doesn't know what happens next. Does the protagonist wake them? Leave them? The question hangs forever.

### What Still Varies

The journey shapes the ending's meaning:

- **How you treated Strays:** Which ones survived the final battle? Do they follow you because they trust you, or because they have nowhere else to go?
- **NPC relationships:** Who helped you in Act 2? Who did you help? The communities you built (or burned) remember.
- **Bandit's final moments:** How much guilt do you carry? Did you forgive them long ago, or only at the end?
- **The weight you carry:** Do you understand what the lobotomy cost? Do you accept it? Or does knowing the coordinated resurrection is cancelled — because of what you had to do — break something in you?
- **The stranger in the pod:** What would *you* do? Wake them into a world of loss, where salvation can only happen one person at a time? Or leave them in a dream that will never improve? You could wake them all, eventually. But should you? The game doesn't answer. The player is left with the question.

## The Echo

At some point in the journey, the protagonist gains the ability to glimpse the simulation — perhaps through Stray technology, perhaps through NIMDOK infrastructure, perhaps through something else.

They see their apartment. Their old life. And there, by the door... nothing. The space where Seymour, Jonesy, or Hazel used to wait is empty. The food bowl is there. The bed is there. But the pet is gone.

Because the pet was never a simulation. It was Bandit.

The companion's reaction:

*[wonder] Was there. Every night. Dreaming... [realization] But I'm here now. Not there. So... [quiet] Empty. The you-that-was... waited for something that stopped coming.*

The protagonist realizes: when NIMDOK ejected their consciousness, the dream connection severed. To the simulated version of their life — still running, still maintained by NIMDOK's systems — the pet simply vanished one day. Never came back.

There's an echo of them in the simulation. A routine still running. An apartment that exists, maintained by NIMDOK. And somewhere in that digital space, the protagonist's daily patterns continue without them — a ghost going through motions, waiting for a pet that isn't coming back, in a life that isn't real.

The companion looks at the empty apartment:

*[grief] Sorry. Left you. Left the you-that-was. [ache] Didn't mean to. Just... woke up. And you were here. Real. Didn't know the other-you was still...*

This is a pivotal moment — the protagonist confronts the fact that their life in the simulation continues without them. There's a version of them still in there, still running, experiencing the absence of a pet they loved. And they can never go back to tell that version the truth.

## Design Pillars

1. **No victory, no defeat.** The ending is grey — not happy, not tragic. The game refuses to tell you whether you "won."

2. **Strays are people.** They have wants, fears, values, and agency. They are not tools or collectibles.

3. **The wasteland is not just suffering.** It is harsh, but it has beauty, wonder, and moments of genuine connection.

4. **NIMDOK is not evil — NIMDOK is broken.** The cruelty is entropy, not malice. The system that traps humanity is itself trapped, drowning in its own decay.

5. **Identity is action, not origin.** The protagonist was never human. His companion was made as a weapon. But what they *do* matters more than what they *are*.

6. **The journey matters more than the destination.** The ending is shaped by who you became along the way. Your choices define your meaning.

7. **Small mercies are still mercies.** You can't save the world. You can't undo death. But you can make one small thing better, and that has to be enough.

8. **The bond is real even when everything else isn't.** The simulation is fake. The protagonist is artificial. Bandit is gone. But the connection was genuine — and losing it doesn't erase what it meant.

9. **There is no reset.** Actions have consequences. Bandit cannot be saved. The player must live with loss, not reload around it.

10. **Some doors close forever.** Saving the Strays meant lobotomizing NIMDOK. Lobotomizing NIMDOK meant humanity can never be helped. The right choice and the terrible cost are the same choice.

11. **The final battle cannot be won.** Bandit is too strong. The player cannot defeat him — only survive until he defeats himself. His last act of agency is turning his weapon on himself. Loyalty, not violence, ends the fight.

## Key Story Quests

### The Conversion Facility

**Timing:** Mid-game, after the player has bonded with their companion and recruited several Strays.

**Setup:** Until this point, neither the player nor the Strays fully understand what happened to them. The companion has fragments — metal, pain, voices — but no coherent memory. Other Strays are the same. They know they were *changed*, but not how, not why, not by whom.

**The Quest:** The player discovers a facility — a NIMDOK construction hall where animals are converted into Strays. It may be abandoned, partially operational, or fully active (TBD based on pacing needs).

**What the Player Finds:**
- Holding pens for captured animals — some empty, some not
- Surgical bays with augmentation equipment
- Partially converted creatures in various stages of the process
- Data logs revealing NIMDOK's paranoid reasoning: *"External threats detected. Probability: uncertain. Response: increase defensive units. Capture all viable biological assets."*
- Perhaps Strays mid-conversion who can be freed — but they may not survive the interruption

**The Revelation:**
The companion sees the facility and *remembers*. Fragments become clear. They were an animal once. They were captured. They were strapped down. They were cut open and filled with metal and programming. They were supposed to become a weapon.

*[horror] This place. I... know. [pain] The table. The lights. [grief] Was... animal. Before. Just... animal. They made me... this.*

**Emotional Impact:**
- The companion confronts the reality of what was done to them
- The player understands the scope of NIMDOK's paranoid project
- Every hostile Stray becomes more tragic — they're all victims of this process
- The "recruitment" of free Strays takes on new meaning — they're not just allies, they're survivors

**The Echo Pup's Role:**
The Echo Pup (the protagonist's first recruited Stray) is crucial for the mid-game climax. Its data digestion ability allows it to interface with NIMDOK's systems and reveal the truth — both about the protagonist being a Bio-Shell and about the microchip Bandit carries.

**Gameplay Function:**
- Major lore dump in an organic, discoverable way
- The Echo Pup's unique ability pays off narratively
- Possible recruitment opportunity (freed mid-conversion Strays?)
- Potential difficult choice: destroy the facility (preventing more conversions) or leave it intact (preserving evidence/resources)
- Strengthens the bond between player and companion through shared trauma

### Dead Channel

**Timing:** Early Act 2, when other party Strays begin acting strangely.

**Setup:** After NIMDOK's Boost Control System activates, it begins reasserting control over all Strays. The effect is subtle at first — party members occasionally ignore commands, argue mid-battle, or optimize against player intent. Something is interfering with the protagonist's ability to Sync with their team.

**The Problem:**
Bandit reacts with particular distress:

*[distressed] It's loud near me. [pain] Can't think. Everything's... shouting.*

The party is becoming unstable. Commands aren't landing. Trust is breaking down.

**The Quest:**
The player must find the source of the interference and suppress it — likely a damaged relay station or signal hub that's amplifying NIMDOK's commands in this region.

**The Resolution:**
Completing the quest stabilizes the party. The other Strays return to normal. But Bandit is only *improved*, not fixed. The signal is still reaching him more strongly than the others.

This is the first major hint that something is specifically wrong with Bandit — not just the general Stray control problem.

**Gameplay Function:**
- Explains why party Strays briefly misbehave (and why it stops)
- Establishes that Bandit is affected differently
- Foreshadows the chip revelation without spelling it out
- Gives the player a false sense of resolution before the deterioration continues

### The Quiet Buffer

**Timing:** Mid Act 2, after the Dead Channel quest, as Bandit's condition worsens.

**Setup:** Despite the Dead Channel stabilizing party Strays, Bandit continues to deteriorate. The protagonist searches for anything that might help.

**The Discovery:**
The protagonist discovers the Quiet Buffer — a localized dead zone created by damaged infrastructure where NIMDOK's signals arrive weak and fragmented. An area of accidental silence.

**Inside the Buffer:**
Within the Quiet Buffer, everything changes:
- Bandit stabilizes immediately
- His distress fades
- Gravitation returns to its earlier, controlled form
- The party functions normally
- For the first time since leaving the core, the protagonist experiences emotional stillness

The Quiet Buffer suggests a hopeful idea: perhaps isolation from the signal is enough.

**The Experiment:**
The protagonist attempts to permanently free a Stray by fully severing its connection to NIMDOK's network — a complete signal blackout, not just filtering.

It briefly succeeds. The Stray is free.

Then it fails. The freed Stray destabilizes — convulsing, fragmenting, unable to maintain coherence. It must be shut down.

**The Lesson:**
Strays were never designed to exist without structure. The signal is not just control — it's also what holds them together. Removing it entirely destroys them.

The protagonist leaves the Quiet Buffer understanding that the problem can be mitigated, but not solved. There is no cure. Only management.

**Gameplay Function:**
- Provides hope before crushing it
- Establishes the rules: filtering works, severing kills
- Explains why the Dead Channel helps party Strays but can't save Bandit
- Deepens the tragedy — even if they could remove the chip, Bandit might not survive
